## SCAM #1: The Double Sale (1:45 - 3:30)

**[VISUAL: Split screen showing same plot being sold to two different buyers, court case files, title documents with same plot number]**

"Scam number one is the oldest trick in the book, but it's still claiming victims every single day: The Double Sale.

Here's how it works: A fraudster obtains genuine title documents—usually through bribery or insider connections at the Lands Registry. They then sell the same plot of land to multiple buyers, sometimes within days of each other. Each buyer gets 'original' documents. Each buyer pays in full. Each buyer thinks they own the land.

**Real Case Study:** In 2023, a plot in Katampe Extension was sold to seven different buyers over a 3-month period. Each buyer paid between ₦45 million and ₦60 million. Total stolen: ₦380 million. The 'seller' disappeared to Dubai. Five of those buyers are still in court today.

**Red Flags:**
- Seller is pushy about quick closing ('Someone else is interested')
- Price is 10-20% below market rate
- Seller refuses to meet at the actual land site
- Documents look 'too perfect' with no wear or aging

**How to Protect Yourself:**
Never buy land without conducting a physical survey and beacon identification. Hire your own independent surveyor—not one recommended by the seller. Cost: ₦150,000 - ₦300,000. Potential savings: ₦50 million+."

## SCAM #2: The Fake C of O (3:30 - 5:15)

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side comparison of fake vs. real C of O, security features highlighted, close-ups of holograms and watermarks]**

"Scam number two is terrifying because of how professional these fakes have become: The Fake Certificate of Occupancy.

Modern counterfeiters aren't using Photoshop and home printers anymore. They're using industrial-grade printing equipment, genuine security paper bought on the black market, and insider knowledge of FCDA document formatting. Some fake C of Os even have valid-looking QR codes that redirect to cloned verification websites.

**How sophisticated are we talking?** In 2024, a syndicate was busted in Wuse that had been operating for 4 years. They had:
- A forged FCDA stamp worth ₦2.5 million
- Genuine security paper from a compromised government supplier
- A fake verification website that looked identical to AGIS
- Collusion with a corrupt lands official who validated documents

**The Tell-Tale Signs:**
1. **Paper quality** — Real C of Os use specific watermarked security paper. Hold it to the light.
2. **Hologram positioning** — Fakes often have holograms slightly off-center
3. **Font inconsistencies** — Check the font weight on official stamps
4. **QR code test** — Scan with your phone. Real ones go to agis.fct.gov.ng. Fakes go to lookalike domains.

**Never rely on visual inspection alone.** Always verify at the FCDA Land Registry. Always."

## SCAM #3: The Estate Allocation Scam (5:15 - 7:00)

**[VISUAL: Aerial view of estates, fake estate signs, victims showing receipts, news reports]**

"Scam number three is the most sophisticated operation I've ever encountered: The Estate Allocation Scam.

This isn't a lone fraudster—this is organized crime. Here's the playbook:

1. **Step 1:** A group identifies a large tract of undeveloped government land or land with disputed ownership
2. **Step 2:** They create a 'development company' with professional branding, websites, and offices in upscale areas like Maitama or Wuse 2
3. **Step 3:** They advertise estate plots at 30-40% below market rates with 'limited time offers'
4. **Step 4:** They show prospective buyers a 'site office' on the land—usually just a temporary container or rented space
5. **Step 5:** They collect deposits from hundreds of buyers, sometimes running into billions of naira
6. **Step 6:** They disappear. The land was never theirs to sell.

**Case Study:** 'Emerald Gardens Estate' in 2022. Professional website, office in Wuse 2, billboards on major roads. Sold 400 plots at ₦8 million each. Total collected: ₦3.2 billion. The 'CEO' fled to Canada. The land? Still government property, completely unsellable.

**Warning Signs:**
- Estate name sounds generic or too good to be true
- Prices significantly below comparable estates
- Pressure to 'buy now before prices increase'
- No verifiable track record of completed estates
- Sales team works on heavy commission and pushes hard

**Verification Steps:**
1. Search CAC for the company registration
2. Check if they have any completed projects you can visit
3. Verify the land status at FCDA (is it actually allocated to this company?)
4. Check court records for any pending litigation
5. Talk to actual residents of their other estates—if they exist"

## SCAM #4: The Omonile/Oluwo Scam (7:00 - 8:30)

**[VISUAL: Traditional settings, baales in robes, juxtaposed with modern fraud scenarios, community meetings]**

"Scam number four preys on cultural respect and tradition: The Fake Omonile or Oluwo Scam.

In Yoruba land tradition, the Omonile (landowners) or Oluwo (village heads) are the custodians of communal land. Buyers often pay 'community fees' to these leaders as part of the land acquisition process.

**The Scam:** Fraudsters impersonate these traditional leaders or create fake 'community associations.' They:
- Set up fake palaces or meeting places
- Hire actors to play the roles of chiefs and elders
- Forge 'community receipts' and 'allocation letters'
- Collect 'community development fees' ranging from ₦500,000 to ₦5 million
- Sell the same land to multiple people

**Why This Works:** Many buyers, especially those in the diaspora, want to respect tradition and ensure their purchase is culturally legitimate. Scammers exploit this goodwill.

**Real Example:** A client in London sent ₦2.3 million in 'community fees' to a 'Baale' in Ibeju-Lekki for a plot that turned out to be government acquisition land. The 'Baale' was a taxi driver from Lagos who rented traditional wear for the video calls.

**Protection Strategy:**
1. Verify the identity of any traditional leader through multiple sources
2. Visit the actual palace or community—not just a rented space
3. Talk to multiple community members independently
4. Cross-check community fees with neighbors who bought land there
5. Ensure traditional receipts are backed by government documentation"

## SCAM #5: The Online/Instagram Scam (8:30 - 9:45)

**[VISUAL: Instagram profiles, WhatsApp conversations, fake testimonials, slick property photos (often stolen from real listings)]**

"Scam number five is exploding right now because of social media: The Online/Instagram Property Scam.

This is how your dream property becomes your worst nightmare:

1. Scammers create professional Instagram pages with beautiful property photos—usually stolen from legitimate agents and developers
2. They run targeted ads to Nigerians in the diaspora and high-income earners locally
3. They use fake testimonials, forged transaction screenshots, and even video 'testimonials' from paid actors
4. They communicate primarily via WhatsApp with convincing fake profiles
5. They request 'holding deposits' or 'survey fees' via mobile transfer—usually ₦200,000 to ₦2 million
6. Once you pay, they block you. The property never existed.

**The Scale:** In 2024, the EFCC reported that social media property scams accounted for ₦1.8 billion in losses. That's just what was reported.

**Spotting the Fake Profiles:**
- Property photos look too perfect (they're often stock photos or stolen from Dubai listings)
- Prices are 40-50% below market
- They refuse video calls or site visits ('I'm traveling,' 'My agent will meet you')
- Urgency tactics ('Price goes up tomorrow,' 'Only 2 plots left')
- No verifiable physical office address
- Recently created account with sudden high activity

**The Golden Rule:** Never send money for land you haven't physically seen and verified. Never. I don't care how convincing the video call is. I don't care how many 'testimonials' they show you. If you can't stand on that land with your own feet and verify the documents at the registry, don't send a kobo."

## CONCLUSION & ANTI-SCAM CHECKLIST (9:45 - 11:00)

**[VISUAL: Checklist graphic, host with checklist document, contact information on screen]**

"Let's recap the 5 scams:
1. Double Sale—same land sold to multiple buyers

3. Estate Allocation—fake development companies
4. Omonile Fraud—fake traditional leaders
5. Online/Instagram—social media bait-and-switch

**But here's the good news:** Every single one of these scams can be avoided with proper verification.

**My Anti-Scam Checklist—Print This Out:**

✅ **Physical Site Visit** — Stand on the land. Confirm beacons. Match boundaries to survey plan.

✅ **Independent Surveyor** — Hire your own. Never use the seller's surveyor.

✅ **Registry Verification** — Verify title at FCDA Land Registry. Cost: ₦50,000. Worth: Everything.

✅ **Company Check** — Search CAC for company status and directors.

✅ **Court Records Search** — Check for pending litigation on the property.

✅ **Community Verification** — If buying community land, verify through multiple sources.

✅ **Never Rush** — Scammers create urgency. Legitimate sellers give you time to verify.

✅ **Use an Escrow Service** — For large transactions, use a lawyer or bank as escrow.

✅ **Payment Trail** — Never pay cash. Use traceable bank transfers.

✅ **Professional Help** — Hire a real estate lawyer. Cost: 1-2% of property value. Protection: Priceless.

**Remember:** The cost of verification is always less than the cost of recovery. A ₦200,000 survey can save you ₦50 million. A ₦100,000 lawyer review can prevent a ₦500 million disaster."
